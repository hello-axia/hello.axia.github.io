Asset Bubbles

By Axia Staff • Aug 2025

Every few years, headlines warn about a “bubble” forming, whether it be in housing, tech stocks, or even NFTs. Then, just as quickly, we hear about the bubble bursting, leaving investors, businesses. And even entire economies are scrambling. But what exactly is an asset bubble? What does it have to do with us ordinary people?

What is an Asset Bubble?

An asset bubble arises when the prices of something, like houses, stocks, NFTs, rises far beyond its actual economic value.

Fair Value vs. Market Price. Normally, an asset’s value reflects its real world usefulness: a house provides shelter, a company sells products that people want, a stock/bond gains value.

In a bubble. Prices rise because people believe they can resell the asset at an even higher price. BUT- the actual value of the asset has not changed.

Ex: In 2021, an NFT sold for ~91.8 million. However, after the NFT market crashed, it was practically worthless.

Ex: The housing market is a common bubble, where people suspect that their houses are worth a lot more than what they are actually valued at.

Eventually, when people realize that the prices can’t keep climbing forever, demand collapses, and the bubble bursts.

Famous Examples:

  • Tulip Mania (1630s). Tulip bulbs in the Netherlands sold for more than a house before collapsing
  • Dot-Com Bubble (1990s). Tech stocks soared on hype about the internet, then crashed when many firms had no real profits.
  • Housing Bubble (2000s). cheap credit and speculation drove home prices to unsustainable levels, triggering the 2008 financial crisis when the bubble burst.

Why do Bubbles occur?

  • Easy Credit. Low interest rates make borrowing cheap, encouraging more buying. (link to interest rate article)
  • Speculation. Investors assume prices will keep rising, so they rush in.
  • Herd Mentality. People buy because others are buying, a positive reinforcement loop.
  • Innovation or Hype. New technologies attract excitement that can inflate prices.

Why do they matter to you?

Even if you are not a stock trader or real estate investors, bubbles will affect you:

  • Housing. A bubble can price families out of homes, then wipe out wealth when it bursts.
  • Jobs. Bursting bubbles hurt businesses. Leading to layoffs.
  • Savings and Retirement. Market downturns hit 401(k)s, pensions, and college savings plans.
  • Economy. Big bubbles can trigger recessions, slowing growth for everyone.

What you can do

  • Diversify. Don’t put all your money in one asset.
  • Think Long-Term. Avoid chasing short term hype: invest in things with real underlying value.
  • Watch for Red Flags. If prices are skyrocketing while fundamentals (profits, rents, wages) don’t match, it could be a bubble.
  • Stay Calm. Market corrections are normal. Don’t panic-sell when prices dips, but be cautious when they’re soaring on hype.

Bottom line

Asset bubbles aren’t a thing of the past, they keep coming back. When they are forming, wealth skyrockets, but when they burst, bankruptcy may occur. The lesson for us is to stay grounded, avoid the herd mentality, and invest with an eye on long-term value. Do not focus on the fads.
