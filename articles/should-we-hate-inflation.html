Inflation: A Double-Edged Sword

By Axia Staff • Aug 15, 2025

Few economic topics stir as much debate as inflation. Why? The effects of inflation are immediately felt. Our daily purchases get more expensive, and we are quick to blame the President, Policy Makers, and big companies. But who really is to blame for inflation, and is it actually bad?

What is inflation?

  • Inflation is the decrease in the purchasing power of the dollar. The same dollar buys less than before.
  • When inflation is high, prices are rising quickly. When inflation is low, prices are rising slowly.

How is it measured?

  • Economists calculate the inflation rate with the Consumer Price Index (CPI), which tracks a “basket” of goods and services (ex: groceries, rent, gas, etc…), which is designed to represent what a typical household spends.
  • The CPI tracks the changing cost of a “basket,” which the Bureau of Labor Statistics updates every month. By comparing the cost of the basket today to the cost in the past, economists can measure how quick prices are rising.
Note

Why CPI might not be accurate

  • The “basket” is an average of what a “typical person” might buy, but not what you may buy.
  • It’s tricky to factor in when products get better.
  • It leaves out some major expenses, like buying a house.
  • It doesn’t account for substitution bias: when price changes, people often change what they buy to cheaper options. Thus, the inflation rate may appear to be higher than it actually is, people are just no longer buying the more expensive options.

What causes inflation?

Inflation occurs when the prices of the goods in the "basket" rises, and this can occur when there is:

  • Consumer spending — Why: When people buy more, stores can raise prices because they do not have enough inventory.
  • Business Spending — Why: When companies invest, they spend more money, therefore increasing costs. Because costs rise for the business, prices rise for the buyers to cover the costs.
  • Government Spending — Why: When the government spends money, it puts more money into the hands of the citizens, increasing the purchases of buyers and giving sellers room to raise prices.
  • Low Interest Rates — When interest rates are higher, people spend less, so lower inflation. When interest rates are lower, people spend more, so high inflation. Read more on Interest Rates here

Remember: Inflation is only a measure of the change in how much a basket of goods cost. These 4 factors all increase the costs of the basket, which will reflect an increase in inflation.

Why inflation can be good

  • Inflation is normal in a functional economy.
  • If there was no inflation, it shows that people are no longer spending money, businesses are no longer developing, and the government is no longer supporting its people.
  • Inflation may reflect a lower unemployment rate.
    • When people are buying, businesses need to hire more workers, which makes jobs easier to find.
      • However, when businesses hire more people, it is “business spending,” so costs of production will go higher. If costs go up for businesses, costs will also increase for buyers, thus an increase in inflation.
  • Inflation encourages people to spend their money now, as it has more purchasing power now than later.
  • Inflation decreases the value of debt owed.
  • Inflation supports wage growth. When employers raise wages in line with inflation, it can help maintain or improve purchasing power if wage growth increases faster than inflation.

Why inflation can be bad

  • Even though controlled amounts of inflation are healthy (the US government aims to maintain 2% annual inflation rate), too much can be harmful.
  • High inflation may lead to:
    • Lower purchasing power
    • Savers being hurt
    • Distorted prices
    • Financial strain of fixed-income households
    • Hyperinflation (money becoming worthless)
  • Most clearly, we, as consumers, feel the impact in the raising of prices. Wages may lag behind, making times of high inflation difficult.

Bottom Line

Inflation is neither purely good nor purely bad- it is a sign of economic activity and improvement. Without inflation, economic stagnation can result in a major economic crash, but too much may bring about the same result. The real goal is stability: keeping inflation predictable so people and businesses can plan and react with confidence.
