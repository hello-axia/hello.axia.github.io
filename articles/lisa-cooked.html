Lisa Cook, Trump, and the Battle Over Fed Interest Rates

By Axia Staff • Aug 21, 2025

Federal Board member Lisa Cook was recently pressured by President Trump to resign her position. Bill Pulte, Director of the Federal Housing Finance Agency has accused her of mortgage fraud (Vox).

While this is happening on the surface, what may be the deeper motives?

Trump has publicly called for interest rate cuts, and by pushing out Cook, who would normally serve until 2038, Trump might be aiming to get more allies into the board (of the Fed) to cut interest rates. This would alter a key lever of economic policy.

What is the state of the economy right now?

Current

  • Inflation: 4.25% to 4.50%, overshooting the Fed’s 2% goal. (Investopedia)
  • Unemployment: near 4.2%, which is close to the 4% average goal. (Morningstar)
  • GDP Growth: modest—estimated at 1–1.5% (article on GDP here)

Fed Goals

  • Inflation Target: 2%
  • Unemployment Goal: ~4%
  • GDP Growth Target: 1.8% to 2.0% a year.

Why Trump Might Want to Cut Interest Rates

Trump wants a significant interest rate cut, from 4% to 1%. (PIIE)

  • Stimulate economic activity: Why? Lower borrowing costs encourages spending money.
  • Lighten fiscal burdens: Why? With government debts above $37 trillion, reducing rates would lower interest payments on that debt. (MarketWatch)
  • Counteract inflationary pressure from tariffs: Why? Trump argues tariffs are pushing prices up, so interest rate cuts could help offset the costs. (AP News)

What Rate Cuts Would Mean Right Now

  • Consumers: Cheaper mortgages and loans
  • Businesses: Lower borrowing costs and reinvigorate investment and hiring
  • Government Finances: Reduced interest costs on federal debt

What are the Risks of lowering Interest Rates?

  • Lowering interest rates could overheat inflation, causing prices to skyrocket. Wages may lag behind the prices, so everything becomes more expensive.
  • It could bring about Asset Bubbles. These may cause things like the 2008 financial crisis. (Read more about Asset Bubbles here)
  • Reduce credibility to the Fed: Fed’s main job is to keep prices stable. If cutting interest rates bring prices up too much, people may lose confidence in the Fed’s commitment to controlling prices.

Why a 3 percent drop from 4.25% to 1.25% Interest Rate Might be Extreme

  • Departure from Normal Policy Adjustments. Typically, the Fed changes interest rates in small increments (0.25–0.5% at a time).
  • Impacts on Savers and Lenders. Interest income for savers, retirees, and institutions would drop significantly. This means all the money you and your family has in savings will not be earning nearly as much as before.
  • Effects on the Dollar and Global Trade. A cut this large tends to weaken the US dollar relative to other countries.
  • Historical Context. Cutting interest rates to 1% are associated with periods of recessions or crises. Cutting to 1% in a non-crisis environment can be viewed as unconventional, without precedence to gauge what might happen.

Bottom line

The economy is at a crossroad: inflation is high, growth is slow, and the unemployment rate is rising. Trump’s push for interest rate cuts shows a desire to reignite the economy. But, the Fed must balance the risks against inflation. The tension between Lisa Cook and Trump may be a physical manifestation of this crossroad.
