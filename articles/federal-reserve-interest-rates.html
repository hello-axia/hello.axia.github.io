The Federal Reserve and Interest Rates

By Axia Staff • Aug 2025

Few institutions have such a direct effect on our wallets as the Federal Reserve. There seems to always be debate amongst experts and the president about what the Federal Reserve should do about interest rates. These results of these decisions are substantial, affecting our mortgage payments, our credit card balances, and even our job prospects. But what exactly does the Federal Reserve do, and why does it matter so much?

What is the Federal Reserve?

The Federal Reserve, often just called “the Fed,” is America’s central Bank. Created in 1913, its job is to keep the economy stable.

The Fed has 2 main goals, known as the dual mandate:

  • Keep prices stable (control inflation)
  • Support maximum employment (make sure people can find jobs)

What are Interest Rates?

Put simply: the interest rate is the price of money. When you borrow, it’s what you pay to borrow someone else’s money. When you save money, it’s what someone else pays you for using your money.

Example:

  • When you put money into a savings account, you are letting the bank lend out your money. But you are not doing this for free, you are charging the bank the interest rate.
  • When you take money out for a loan at the bank, you are taking money from someone else who is saving money, and paying the bank the interest rate.

The Fed set the federal funds rate, which is the rate banks charge each other for short-term loans.

Why does this matter? Once the Fed changes its rate, it affects everything: mortgages, car loans, college loans, credit cards, and business investments.

How the Fed Uses Interest Rates

Think of interest rates as the Fed’s main “lever” to steer the economy:

When the Fed Raises Rates

  • Borrowing becomes more expensive
    Why? You have to pay more to borrow money.
  • People and businesses spend less
    Why? It is more profitable to put your money in savings as you will get more return on lending the money to others.
  • Inflation slows down
    Why? People are putting more money into savings, therefore spending less.
  • Growth slows down
    Why? People are spending less money, which slows inflation. (read more on inflation here)

When the Fed Lowers Rates

  • Borrowing becomes cheaper
    Why? You pay less to borrow money.
  • People and businesses spend more
    Why? It is less profitable to put your money in savings, as you will get less return on lending your money to others. Might as well spend it now!
  • Inflation speeds up
    Why? People are spending more money, which drives inflation!
  • Growth speeds up
    Why? People are spending more money, which drives business and lowers unemployment.

Why it Matters for You

  • Consumers: A rate cut could lower your mortgage or credit card interest, thereby giving you more money. A higher rate makes your loans more expensive
  • Businesses: A higher rate means higher borrowing costs, making expansion or hiring harder
  • Markets: Stocks often rise when rates fall (cheap credit fuels growth) but fall when rates climb
  • Global Trade: Lower US rates weaken the dollar because it drives inflation, making exports cheaper abroad but imports more expensive in the US.

Risks and Trade-Offs

The Fed has to walk on a tightrope:

  • Too high for too long(saving is more beneficial): may lead to Recession and layoffs
  • Too low for too long(spending is more beneficial): inflation, bubbles in housing or stocks, and unstable growth.

This balancing act is why Fed decisions are so closely watched. Small changes affect the entire economy.

Bottom Line

The Federal Reserve does not control the economy, but it sets the tone for it. By affecting the interest rates, it tries to keep the US on a path of steady growth without letting inflation spiral out of control. For ordinary people, every Fed decision matters. It affects how much we pay at the store and whether we can afford a home.
